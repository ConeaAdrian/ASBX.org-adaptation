Mesothelioma Overview

Mesothelioma is a highly aggressive and lethal cancer that develops in the mesothelium, a thin tissue layer covering most of our internal organs. While treatments exist, a full cure is often elusive for many mesothelioma patients.

Types of Mesothelioma

Medical professionals categorize mesothelioma based on the affected mesothelium. The most common type is pleural mesothelioma, which affects the tissue surrounding the lungs. Other less common types include:

  • Peritoneal mesothelioma - affects abdominal tissue

  • Pericardial mesothelioma - affects the heart's surrounding tissue

  • Mesothelioma of tunica vaginalis - affects the tissue around the testicles

Symptoms of Mesothelioma

The symptoms associated with mesothelioma vary depending on the cancer's location.

Pleural Mesothelioma

  • Chest pain

  • Painful coughing

  • Shortness of breath

  • Unusual tissue lumps under the chest skin

  • Unexplained weight loss

Peritoneal Mesothelioma

  • Abdominal pain

  • Abdominal swelling

  • Nausea

  • Unexplained weight loss

Other Mesotheliomas

The symptoms for other, rarer types of mesothelioma, like pericardial mesothelioma and mesothelioma of the tunica vaginalis, remain largely unclear due to their rarity.

Causes of Mesothelioma

Cancer typically arises from a series of changes or mutations in a cell's DNA. These mutations instruct the cell to grow and multiply uncontrollably, leading to tumor formation. While the initial triggers for these mutations remain unclear in the case of mesothelioma, it's thought that a combination of factors like inherited conditions, the environment, health conditions, and lifestyle choices play a part.

Mesothelioma Risk Factors

Mesothelioma is primarily linked to asbestos exposure. Asbestos, a natural heat-resistant mineral, is widely used in numerous products such as insulation materials, brakes, shingles, and flooring due to its strong fibers. However, when disturbed, such as during mining or insulation removal, asbestos can create dust that, when inhaled or ingested, may settle in the lungs or stomach. These embedded fibers can cause irritation leading to the development of mesothelioma, although the exact mechanism is still unclear. The latency period between exposure and disease manifestation can range from 20 to 60 years.

While many individuals exposed to asbestos never develop mesothelioma, it suggests the involvement of other factors in determining susceptibility to the disease. It may be the case that certain people have inherited a genetic predisposition to cancer, or there may be other conditions that augment their risk.

Several factors can escalate the risk of mesothelioma:

  1. Prior asbestos exposure: Individuals who've been directly exposed to asbestos fibers at their workplace or home have a substantially heightened risk of developing mesothelioma.
  2. Residential proximity to an asbestos worker: Asbestos workers may bring home the mineral's fibers on their skin or clothing, potentially exposing household members to the mineral over prolonged periods. This indirect exposure can also increase the risk of developing mesothelioma. However, the risk can be mitigated by adopting preventive measures, like showering and changing clothes before leaving the workplace.
  3. Family history of mesothelioma: Those with a close relative, such as a parent, sibling, or child diagnosed with mesothelioma, may face an elevated risk of the disease.
  4. Prior chest radiation therapy: Those who underwent radiation therapy for chest cancer may also have an increased likelihood of developing mesothelioma.

Complications

As pleural mesothelioma progresses, it applies pressure on the chest area's structures, leading to complications like difficulty breathing, chest pain, trouble swallowing, nerve and spinal cord pressure, and fluid accumulation in the chest.

Prevention

Lowering your asbestos exposure can reduce your mesothelioma risk. Here are some preventive measures:

  • Be aware if your work involves asbestos exposure.

  • Follow all workplace safety regulations.

  • Implement safe practices around asbestos in your home. Do not attempt to remove asbestos on your own; instead, hire a qualified expert.
